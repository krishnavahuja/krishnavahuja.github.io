About our State
Manipur, a picturesque state nestled in the northeastern region of India, with the city of Imphal located in the centre of the state as its capital is a land of breathtaking beauty, rich cultural heritage, and vibrant traditions. With its lush green valleys, serene lakes, cascading waterfalls, and mist-covered hills, Manipur is a paradise for nature lovers. The name Manipur means “land of gems”. It is bounded by the Indian states of Nagaland to the north, Mizoram to the south and Assam to the west. It also borders two regions
of Myanmar, Sagaing Region to the east and Chin State to the south. The official and most widely spoken language is Meitei language. Manipur has been at the crossroads of Asian economic and cultural exchange for more than 2,500 years. It connects the Indian subcontinent and Central Asia to Southeast Asia, East Asia, Siberia, regions in the Arctic, Micronesia and Polynesia enabling migration of people, cultures and religions. Its economy centres on agriculture and forestry, and trade and cottage industries also are important. Area - 8,621 square miles (22,327 square km). Population - (2001) 2,293,896; (2011) 2,855,794.

History of Manipur
Manipur's history is extensive and fascinating, spanning several centuries and including a variety of stories, kingdoms, and noteworthy occasions. The region has experienced the emergence of colonial control, the rise and fall of several dynasties, and the fight for freedom.

 Ancient Origins:
The origins of Manipur date back to ancient times. According to folklore, Manipur was believed to have been a lake called Loktak, which was converted into a valley by Lord Pakhangba, the mythical progenitor of the Meitei people. The Meitei kingdom emerged around the 1st century CE and flourished as a sovereign state for centuries.

 The Kingdom of Manipur:
Manipur's recorded history began with the advent of the Ningthouja dynasty in the 15th century. The kingdom witnessed a period of growth and prosperity under various rulers. It maintained trade relations with neighboring kingdoms and enjoyed cultural exchanges with Tibet, Myanmar, and Southeast Asia.

The 18th century marked a significant turning point in Manipur's history. The kingdom faced invasions from external forces, particularly from the Burmese (present-day Myanmar). Manipur witnessed several wars and the temporary occupation of the Burmese. However, in 1826, Manipur regained independence with the help of the British after the First Anglo-Burmese War.
Colonial Rule and Struggle for Independence:
The 19th century saw Manipur fall under the sphere of British influence. The British East India Company established political control, leading to the signing of the Treaty of Yandabo in 1826. Manipur became a princely state, and the British introduced administrative reforms.

During the British era, Manipur faced challenges related to land disputes, socio-economic changes, and political unrest. In 1891, a major conflict known as
the Anglo-Manipur War erupted when the Manipuri king opposed British interference in the state's administration. The war resulted in the deposition of the monarchy and the annexation of Manipur by the British Empire.

The struggle for independence gained momentum in the early 20th century. Leaders like Bir Tikendrajit and Thangal General spearheaded movements against British colonial rule. The Manipur State Durbar, a representative body, was established in 1947, paving the way for the state's eventual merger with the newly independent India in 1949.

 Post-Independence:
After joining India, Manipur became a Union Territory in 1956 and eventually attained full statehood in 1972. Manipur faced challenges related to insurgency, political conflicts, and socio-economic development in the subsequent decades. However, efforts have been made to promote peace, stability, and inclusive growth in the state.

Today, Manipur stands as a vibrant state, embracing its diverse cultural heritage and striving for progress. It remains a symbol of resilience, as its people continue to preserve their traditions, artistic expressions, and unique identity in the face of modernity.
The history of Manipur is a testament to the indomitable spirit of its people and their enduring quest for freedom, preservation of culture, and pursuit of a better future.

Art and Culture
Manipur has a lively and affluent artistic legacy. The history, mythology, and religious practises of Manipur are all strongly ingrained in its artistic traditions. The culture of Manipur is renowned for its distinctive music, dancing, crafts, and festivals, which showcase the people's artistic talent and traditional values. -

Dance:
Manipuri dance is one of the most famous art forms of the state. It is characterized by graceful movements, intricate footwork, and expressive hand gestures. The dance form is often associated with the Ras Lila, a genre of dance-drama that depicts the love story of Lord Krishna and Radha. The traditional costumes, including brightly colored skirts (Phaneks) for women and dhotis for men, add to the visual splendor of the performances.
Music:
 Manipuri music is an important part of the state's cultural heritage. The main musical instruments used in Manipuri music are the flute, the Pung (a barrel-shaped drum), and the Kartal (cymbals). The tunes frequently have a calming, melodic quality that reflects the area's strong spiritual and religious roots. Manipuri music is fundamentally composed of both folk tunes and classical pieces.

 Textiles and Handicrafts:
Manipur is well known for its excellent textiles and handicrafts. The region is renowned for manufacturing fine silk textiles, such as the well-known Manipuri Moirangphee and Wangkhei Phee. These textiles include elaborate designs and motifs and are weaved using conventional methods. The skilled weavers of Manipur have preserved this ancient art form and continue to create beautiful textiles.

Pottery and Crafts:
The pottery of Manipur is highly regarded for its unique designs and craftsmanship. In particular, the village of Longpi is famous for its black pottery which uses ground black serpentinite rock mixed with special clay. In the same way, Manipur is known for its bamboo and cane craft where skilled craftsmen use traditional techniques to manufacture a variety of products such as baskets, furniture or decorative objects.

Festivals:
Manipur celebrates a number of colorful festivals throughout the year. The Manipuri New Year, known as Cheiraoba, is an important festival that marks the beginning of the lunar calendar. The Lai Haraoba festival is a religious celebration where traditional rituals, music, and dance performances are held to honor the deities of Manipur. The Yaoshang festival, celebrated during the spring season, is a vibrant and joyful festival featuring traditional dances, music, and sports activities.

Martial Arts:
Manipur is the birthplace of several ancient martial arts forms, including Thang-Ta and Sarit Sarak. Thang-Ta is a combination of sword and spear fighting techniques, while Sarit Sarak focuses on hand-to-hand combat. These martial arts forms not only serve as self-defense techniques but also carry cultural and historical significance.

Cuisine:
Manipuri cuisine is known for its unique flavors and use of local ingredients. Rice is the staple food, and the cuisine often includes dishes like Eromba (a spicy stew), Singju (a vegetable salad), and Kangshoi (a mixed vegetable soup). The cuisine of Manipur reflects the diverse cultural influences from neighboring states and countries like Myanmar and China.



The art and culture of Manipur are deeply ingrained in the daily lives of its people. These artistic expressions serve as a means of preserving traditions, promoting harmony, and celebrating the cultural identity of the state.

Wonders of Manipur
Kangla Fort:
An historic fortified palace in Imphal, India's Manipur state, is called the Kangla Fort. Originally located on both sides (western and eastern) of the bank of the Imphal River, it is now only partially intact on the western side. In ancient Meetei, kangl signifies "the dominant portion of the dry land." It served as the historical residence of Manipur's Meetei kings. The historical capital of Manipur before modern times was Kangla (Imphal).For the people of Manipur, the Kangla is a sacred location that serves as a reminder of their former freedom. The Meiteis see it as a holy location.


Imphal War Cemetry:
The Imphal War Cemetery is located in Imphal, the capital of the Indian state of Manipur, in Northeast India, which has an international border with upper Burma (now Myanmar). The cemetery has 1,600 Commonwealth burials of the Second World War and is maintained by the Commonwealth War Graves Commission. Many of the people buried in the cemetery were killed during the Battle of Kohima and Imphal.


Loktak Lake:
Loktak Lake is a freshwater lake located in Moirang in Manipur state, India It is the largest freshwater lake in South Asia. It is a pulsating lake, with a surface area varying from 250 sq km to 500 sq km during the rainy season with a typical area of 287 sq km. The etymology of Loktak is Lok = "stream" and tak = "the end" in Meitei language (Manipuri language). It is famous for the phumdi (heterogeneous mass of vegetation, soil and organic matter at various
stages of decomposition) floating over it. The largest of all the phumdis covers an area of 40 km2 (15 sq mi) and is situated on the southeastern shore of the lake. Located on this phumdi, Keibul Lamjao National Park is the only floating national park in the world. The park is the last natural refuge of the endangered Sangai (state animal), Rucervus eldii eldii or Manipur brow-antlered deer (Cervus eldi eldi), one of three subspecies of Eld's deer.
Keibul Lamjao National Park:
The Keibul Lamjao National Park is a national park in the Bishnupur district of the state of Manipur in Northeast India. It is 40 km2 (15.4 sq mi) in area, the only floating national park in the world, and an integral part of Loktak Lake.
